Just Exactly What is Gluten?

Gluten is a group of proteins found in cereal grains such as wheat, barley, and rye. It helps give structure and elasticity to dough and is commonly found in many breads, pastas, and baked goods. However, some individuals are allergic or intolerant to gluten, which can cause a range of health issues, such as celiac disease, gluten sensitivity, or wheat allergy.

Why go on a Gluten Free Diet?

Pros:

  1. May relieve symptoms of celiac disease and gluten sensitivity
  2. lead to an increase in nutrient-dense whole foods, such as fruits and vegetables
  3. Can lead to weight loss as gluten-free foods often contain fewer calories and less sugar and fat than their gluten-containing counterparts

Cons:

  1. Can be more expensive as gluten-free products are often pricier than regular products
  2. May be lower in fiber, iron, and B vitamins due to the removal of fortified grains and cereals
  3. May limit food choices and make it more challenging to eat out or find convenient, on-the-go options.

Tips on becoming Gluten-Free:

  • Educate yourself on what foods and products contain gluten. Read food labels carefully before purchasing.
  • Focus on naturally gluten-free foods such as fruits, vegetables, lean proteins, and whole grains like quinoa and brown rice.
  • Find gluten-free alternatives for your favorite foods, such as pasta made from brown rice or quinoa.
  • Look for restaurants that offer gluten-free options, or call ahead to ensure that they can accommodate your dietary needs.
  • Be patient with yourself and don't expect to make the transition overnight. It may take time to adjust to a new way of eating.
  • Join a support group or online forum to connect with others who are following a gluten-free lifestyle. You can learn from their experiences, share resources, and find encouragement.

Additional Resources on Gluten free Living

Tips on Gluten Free from BBC

The main, most self-explanatory food allergy to watch out for while on a gluten-free diet is wheat. There is no simple substitution for wheat, but baked-goods can be produced using non-wheat flours such as rice, sorghum, soy, tapioca or potato starch. Foods made with other grains such as rice, quinoa and oats are great gluten-free alternatives!
